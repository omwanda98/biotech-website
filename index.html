Steps to use the CRISPR-CAS9 System

This is a gene editing technology that uses a combination of a bacterial protein called Cas9 and a guide RNA (gRNA) to make precise cuts in DNA. The system is derived from a natural defense mechanism that bacteria use to defend against viruses..

STEP 1

Select an organism for the experiment: Before beginning a CRISPR-CAS9 experiment, the researcher needs to select the organism they wish to study. The organism must have a genome which is compatible with the CRISPR-CAS9 system.

STEP 2

Select a gene of the target location: After selecting the organism, the researcher needs to identify the gene which they wish to edit. This can be done by searching gene databases or literature.

STEP 3

Select a CRISPR-CAS9 system: The researcher needs to select a CRISPR-CAS9 system to use. This can be purchased from a supplier or constructed in-house.

STEP 4

Select and Design the sgRNA: The researcher needs to select and design a single-guide RNA (sgRNA) which will target the desired gene. This will be used to direct the Cas9 enzyme to the correct location for editing.

STEP 5

Synthesizing and cloning of sgRNA: The researcher needs to synthesize and clone the sgRNA from the design. This can be done using standard molecular biology techniques or purchased from a supplier.

STEP 6,7,8

Delivering the sgRNA and CAS9. Validating the experiment . Finally Culturing the altered cells. The researcher needs to culture the cell line which has been altered by the CRISPR-CAS9 system.

Ethics

The ethics associated with CRISPR are complex and multifaceted. One of the most prominent ethical considerations is the potential for the misuse of CRISPR technology, such as gene-editing tools, to create so-called “designer” babies. Additionally, ethical considerations must be taken into account when using CRISPR to modify crops or animals, as there is the potential for unintended consequences that could have a negative impact on the environment. Finally, ethical considerations must also be considered when using CRISPR in medical applications, as its use could result in unintended human genome changes.

Ethical issues related to CRISPR gene editing technology

Safety: The safety of CRISPR gene editing is a significant concern, especially in the context of human gene editing. The risk of off-target mutations and other unintended consequences is a major concern. Equity: There is a risk that gene editing could be used to create genetic "superiority" and exacerbate existing social inequalities. There are also concerns about access to gene editing technology and the potential for it to become available only to those who can afford it. Informed consent: In the context of human gene editing, informed consent is critical. There are concerns that patients may not fully understand the risks and benefits of the procedure or that they may be coerced into undergoing gene editing. Genetic diversity: There is a concern that gene editing could lead to a loss of genetic diversity, which is essential for the resilience and adaptation of populations to changing environments. Unintended consequences: CRISPR technology is still in its infancy, and there is a risk that unintended consequences could arise from gene editing, such as the creation of new diseases or the disruption of ecosystems. Dual-use: The CRISPR technology has the potential to be used for both beneficial and harmful purposes, and there is a concern that it could be used for biological warfare or terrorism.
